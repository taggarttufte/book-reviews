# Book Review Writing Guide

Target: ~1 review per day, 800-1,500 words each.
Deadline: soft goal of finishing the list within 10-14 days.
If a review isn't flowing, skip it and come back later.

---

## Review Structure (loose, not mandatory)

1. **Opening** (1-2 paragraphs) -- What is this book about, in your words? Not a summary. What's the central claim or tension?
2. **What changed your thinking** (2-4 paragraphs) -- The core of the review. What did you believe before, and what shifted? What stuck with you months later?
3. **Where it falls short** (1-2 paragraphs) -- Shows critical thinking. Every book has weaknesses. What didn't land, what was missing, what would you push back on?
4. **The bigger picture** (1-2 paragraphs) -- How does this connect to other things you've read, your career direction, or ideas you're wrestling with? This is where your unique perspective shows.

Don't feel locked into this. Some reviews will be mostly section 2. Some will be mostly section 4. Follow what you actually want to say.

---

## Suggested Order

Start with the books you have the strongest reactions to, not the "most important" ones.

### Week 1 (strongest recall, most to say)

**Day 1: The Stranger -- Albert Camus**
Short book, focused thesis, directly tied to your philosophical framework.

- What does Meursault's indifference actually mean? Is he honest or broken?
- The courtroom scene: he's condemned not for murder but for not crying at his mother's funeral. What does that say about society's relationship to performed emotion?
- Camus said Meursault is "the only Christ we deserve." What did he mean?
- How does this connect to your shift away from Christianity? Did the book hit differently because of that timing?
- The famous last line about the "gentle indifference of the world." Is that liberating or terrifying? You've landed on liberating (from your journal). Why?
- Connection to AI doom: if the universe is indifferent, does that make alignment work more or less meaningful?

**Day 2: Atlas Shrugged -- Ayn Rand**
Formative book. Don't defend or apologize for liking it. Explain what it gave you.

- What specifically resonated at the age you read it? What need did it meet?
- Galt's speech: did you actually sit through all of it? What landed and what didn't?
- The "makers vs. takers" framing is reductive. Do you still buy it, or has your thinking evolved?
- How does Rand's extreme individualism interact with your current interest in alignment (which is fundamentally a collective action problem)?
- Dagny Taggart as a character: what makes her compelling vs. the male protagonists?
- Controversial take: what's the strongest criticism of this book that you think has merit?
- Has Taleb, Camus, or anyone else you've read since complicated your relationship with Rand?

**Day 3: The Fountainhead -- Ayn Rand**
Pair with Atlas Shrugged but a different book.

- How is Roark different from Galt? Which character do you find more compelling and why?
- The Toohey vs. Roark dynamic: is Toohey a realistic villain or a strawman?
- Roark dynamiting Cortlandt: justified or insane? Where do you land?
- The book's thesis that integrity means never compromising your vision. Do you still believe that fully, or has experience added nuance?
- Rand's architecture metaphor: does "building something true to your vision" map onto your approach to ML projects? Balatro RL went through 5 iterations -- Roark wouldn't have iterated. Is iteration compromise or growth?
- What do people get wrong about this book when they dismiss it?

**Day 4: The Maniac -- Benjamin Labatut**
Directly AI-relevant. Von Neumann's legacy.

- Labatut blends fiction and history. Did that enhance or undermine the book for you?
- Von Neumann's deathbed conversion to Catholicism: what do you make of that, given your own move away from religion?
- The book implies that the architects of our computational world were often unstable, obsessive, or morally ambiguous. Does that matter for how we think about AI?
- The connection between game theory, nuclear deterrence, and AI: von Neumann was involved in all three. What pattern does Labatut see?
- How does this compare to the Isaacson biographies (Jobs, Musk) in how it portrays genius?
- The title "The Maniac" -- who is it actually referring to? Von Neumann, or the machine?

**Day 5: The Player of Games -- Iain M. Banks**
AI governance fiction before the field existed.

- The Culture is run by superintelligent AIs (Minds) that are genuinely benevolent. Is Banks being naive, or is he imagining what aligned AI actually looks like?
- Gurgeh's relationship with the Minds: he's a human in a post-scarcity society run by AI. Is he free or a pet?
- The game of Azad as a metaphor for civilization: the idea that how a society plays games reveals what it values. Does this hold up?
- Banks wrote this in 1988. What did he get right about AI that contemporary thinkers missed?
- How does the Culture's approach to AI governance compare to what alignment researchers propose today?
- The ending and what it reveals about the Minds' actual level of manipulation. Were they aligned or just effective at hiding their agenda?

### Week 2 (still strong, slightly less urgent)

**Day 6: Sapiens -- Yuval Noah Harari**
Big-picture civilizational thinking.

- Harari's claim that shared fictions (money, nations, religion) are what enable large-scale human cooperation. Do you buy it?
- The agricultural revolution as "history's biggest fraud." Compelling or overblown?
- How does Harari's framework for understanding human cooperation apply to the alignment problem? Is "aligning AI" just another shared fiction we need to construct?
- Where does Harari oversimplify? He's been criticized for being a mile wide and an inch deep.
- Did this book change any specific belief you held, or did it mostly organize things you already suspected?

**Day 7: Fooled by Randomness -- Nassim Nicholas Taleb**
Complete the Taleb pair with Black Swan.

- How is this different from The Black Swan? What does it add that Black Swan doesn't cover?
- The "dentist vs. trader" distinction: systematic skill vs. lucky outcomes dressed up as skill. Where does ML research fall on this spectrum?
- Taleb's contempt for backtesting and historical data in finance. Does the same criticism apply to ML benchmarks?
- Survivorship bias in AI research: we see the papers that worked, not the 100 that didn't. How does Taleb's framework apply?
- Is Taleb's writing style an asset or a liability? You mentioned at 2.7x it was manageable.

**Day 8: Steve Jobs + Elon Musk (combined review)**
What founder biographies reveal about obsessive optimization.

- What do Jobs and Musk have in common that matters? What's different?
- Isaacson's approach: he clearly admires both while documenting their cruelty. Does he let them off too easy?
- The "reality distortion field" -- is this a superpower or a pathology? Can you learn it or is it innate?
- Both men optimized ruthlessly for their vision. How does that compare to Rand's Roark? To your own approach to projects?
- Jensen Huang (from your journal): you noted his worldview seemed genuine over 3 hours but his incentive is to sell GPUs. Same filter applies to Isaacson's subjects?
- What did these books teach you about what you want from a career vs. what you don't?

**Day 9: Cryptonomicon -- Neal Stephenson**
Only if you have something to say beyond "I enjoyed it."

- Stephenson's vision of crypto/privacy in 1999 vs. what actually happened. What did he get right?
- The WWII/modern parallel structure: does it work or does it bloat the book?
- Bobby Shaftoe vs. Randy Waterhouse as two modes of engaging with the world (physical action vs. intellectual systems). Which do you identify with?
- The book is obsessed with information theory and encryption. How does that connect to your math background?
- If this review doesn't flow, skip it. No shame.

## Tips

- **Write ugly first drafts.** Don't edit as you go. Get thoughts down, clean up after.
- **Start with the sentence you're most excited to write**, not the introduction. The intro is usually the last thing that clicks.
- **If you're stuck, answer one of the questions above out loud** (voice memo on your phone). Then transcribe and clean up.
- **Your philosophical journal entries are rough drafts already.** For Camus, Taleb, and Callard, you've already done most of the thinking. The review is just organizing it for an audience.
- **Don't summarize the plot.** Assume the reader either has read the book or can look up a summary. Your job is to say what the book *means*, not what *happens*.

## Adding to the Site

When a review is ready, create a file in `_reviews/` with this template:

```markdown
---
title: "Book Title"
book_author: "Author Name"
date: 2026-04-09
date_finished: 2026-03-09    # when you finished reading (optional)
date_reviewed: 2026-04-09    # when you wrote the review (optional)
rating: 5
summary: "One sentence for the index page."
tags: [AI-safety, philosophy-of-science, epistemology]
---

Your review here...
```

`date` drives sorting and the URL/file naming. `date_finished` and `date_reviewed` are optional but useful context for retroactive reviews — when both are present, the review page shows "Finished X · Reviewed Y" instead of the single date.

Then:
```bash
cd C:\Users\Taggart\clawd\book-reviews
git add -A
git commit -m "Review: Book Title"
git push
```

Site updates automatically in ~60 seconds.
